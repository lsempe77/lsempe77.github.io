13  Small-n Evaluation as Causal Inquiry

Development programmes routinely operate at scales that defeat standard statistical inference. A ministry reform has one treated unit. A district health pilot covers four sites. A training cohort enrols fifteen participants. The publication trends in Chapter 1 show that evaluators are responding: the volume of work on process tracing, QCA, realist evaluation, and single-case designs has grown sharply since 2010. But growth in volume has not been matched by growth in conceptual integration. Methods have developed within separate disciplinary traditions — clinical psychology for SCEDs, comparative politics for QCA, public health for realist evaluation — and practitioners still lack a shared vocabulary for explaining why they chose one approach over another and what kind of causal warrant it provides.

This book proposed a vocabulary. The eight logics of causal inference (Chapter 3) name the distinct ways in which an evaluator can justify a causal claim when cross-case statistical comparison is unavailable or insufficient. Six of those logics received dedicated treatment in Part II: counterfactual reasoning through within-unit temporal comparison (Chapter 4), multiple-causation reasoning through configurational methods (Chapter 5), generative reasoning through process tracing, contribution analysis, and realist evaluation (Chapter 6), diagnostic reasoning through Bayesian updating (Chapter 7), participatory reasoning through stakeholder-validated causal narratives (Chapter 8), and simulated reasoning through computational modelling (Chapter 9). Two logics — regularity and manipulation — were treated briefly because their small-n applicability is narrower, though both remain present as components of other methods.

The framework does not rank these logics. Each answers a different causal question. An anti-corruption evaluation that asks “did the reform reduce fraud?” needs counterfactual or diagnostic evidence. The same evaluation, reframed as “why did the reform work in three provinces but not in two?”, needs configurational evidence. Reframed again as “how did the reform change bureaucratic behaviour?”, it needs generative evidence. These are not competing answers to the same question. They are answers to different questions, and the decision matrix (Chapter 10) and integration protocols (Chapter 11) help the evaluator decide which questions to ask and how to combine the logics that answer them.

The framework also does not claim that small-n methods are interchangeable with large-n methods. They answer different questions. A cluster-randomised trial of a conditional cash transfer programme estimates the average treatment effect across hundreds of communities. A process trace in one community explains how the transfer changed household decision-making. A QCA across fifteen communities identifies which combinations of programme features and contextual conditions produced the outcome. These are not competing answers to the same question. They are answers to different questions, each valid on its own terms and more valuable in combination.

The CCT example is well documented across all three logics. Schultz (2004) estimates the average effect of Mexico’s Progresa using experimental data. Adato & Hoddinott (2010) trace the mechanisms through which CCTs change household behaviour. Fiszbein & Schady (2009) synthesise the configurational evidence on which programme features and contexts produce enrolment gains.

13.1 A research agenda

Four gaps deserve attention.

First, the field lacks formal frameworks for meta-inference: combining evidence generated by different logics into a coherent causal account. The integration chapter (Chapter 11) described sequential, concurrent, and nested designs, but these are procedural arrangements, not inferential frameworks. When a QCA result and a process-tracing finding point in different directions, how should the evaluator weight the two? When a SCED effect estimate is paired with an MSC story, what is the combined evidential warrant? These questions require formal development that goes beyond the scope of this book.

Second, software platforms for small-n evaluation remain fragmented. QCA has dedicated packages (fsQCA, the QCA package in R). Bayesian process tracing has prototype tools but no widely adopted platform. Agent-based modelling and system dynamics have their own environments (NetLogo, Vensim). No integrated platform allows an evaluator to move between logics within a single analytical workflow. Building such a platform would lower the practical barriers to multi-logic evaluation.

Third, systematic reviews of small-n impact evaluations in development are scarce. The co-occurrence analysis in Chapter 11 showed which methods are discussed together in the published literature, but it did not assess how well those methods are applied. Preliminary evidence from an evidence gap map of over 5,000 empirical studies in fragile and conflict-affected settings suggests that specialised small-n methods remain rare even in contexts where small samples are the norm: process tracing, QCA, contribution analysis, and Bayesian methods together accounted for fewer than 60 of the 5,327 studies examined (Sempé, 2026). How often do QCA studies in development evaluation follow established transparency standards? How often do process-tracing evaluations pre-register their causal claims? How often do participatory evaluations include independent substantiation? Answering these questions requires a systematic review that codes evaluations against the quality standards proposed in Chapter 12.

Fourth, evaluation training and curricula have not kept pace with the expansion of the methods repertoire. Most evaluation courses still organise methods by tradition (quantitative versus qualitative) rather than by causal logic. A logic-based curriculum would start with the question “what kind of causal claim do you want to make?” and work outwards to the methods that support that claim, rather than starting with the method and hoping the right question emerges.

These four gaps map directly onto the pathways of change identified in the EIPM framework (Sempé et al., 2025). Meta-inference frameworks and training reform address the capabilities pathway, strengthening evaluators’ and commissioners’ ability to generate and appraise multi-logic evidence. Integrated software platforms address the structures and processes pathway, embedding methodological rigour in the tools that evaluators use. Systematic reviews address the evidence culture pathway, establishing community standards for how small-n methods should be applied and evaluated. Progress on any one pathway reinforces the others, and advance on all four would move small-n evaluation from a specialist niche into the routine methodological repertoire of the evidence-to-policy ecosystem.
