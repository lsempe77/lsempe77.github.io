12  Bias, Transparency, and Quality Standards

Every evaluation method is vulnerable to bias. At large n, a biased observation is diluted by the rest of the sample. At small n, each case, each interview, each data point carries more inferential weight, and a single biased input can shift the conclusion. The quality challenge in small-n evaluation is not that bias is more frequent, but that it is less forgiving. This chapter maps the main sources of bias, proposes mitigation strategies, and reviews reporting standards that apply across the logics covered in this book.

12.1 Sources of bias at small n

White & Phillips (2012) identified three families of bias that affect small-n impact evaluations. Each operates differently depending on the logic being used, but all three are present across methods.

Respondent biases arise when the people providing evidence distort it, whether deliberately or unconsciously. Social desirability bias leads respondents to report what they think the evaluator wants to hear. Recall bias distorts accounts of past events, especially when participants are asked to reconstruct causal sequences after the fact. Attribution inflation leads stakeholders to over-attribute observed changes to the programme, either because they want the programme to continue or because the programme is the most salient recent event in their experience. These biases are most acute for participatory methods (Chapter 8), where the causal evidence depends on stakeholder testimony, but they also affect process tracing when key informant interviews form the primary evidence base.

Evaluator biases arise from the analyst’s own reasoning processes. Confirmation bias leads evaluators to seek and weight evidence that supports their prior beliefs about the programme’s effectiveness. In Bayesian process tracing (Chapter 7), Befani (2020) identified confirmation bias as a specific risk: evaluators may set priors too high or interpret ambiguous evidence as confirming the hypothesis. Anchoring bias leads evaluators to fix on early evidence and adjust insufficiently as new evidence arrives. The availability heuristic leads evaluators to overweight vivid or recent evidence relative to systematic but less memorable data. These biases are present across all logics but are hardest to detect in methods that rely on qualitative judgement, precisely because the judgement is exercised by the person who is also responsible for the evaluation’s conclusions.

Selection bias arises from the choice of cases, sites, or units. At small n, case selection is an analytical decision rather than a sampling procedure, and the wrong choice can predetermine the finding. Selecting on the dependent variable (choosing only cases where the programme succeeded) inflates the apparent effectiveness of the intervention. Convenience sampling (choosing cases that are easy to access) introduces systematic differences between the studied cases and the population of interest. In configurational methods (Chapter 5), the choice of cases determines the truth table and can affect which solutions the analysis identifies.

12.2 Mitigation strategies

Four strategies reduce the risk of bias in small-n evaluations, though none eliminates it entirely.

Structured protocols constrain the evaluator’s discretion at each step. A pre-specified theory of change, agreed before data collection begins, defines which mechanisms the evaluator will investigate and which evidence would confirm or disconfirm each one. Coding rubrics specify how evidence is classified. Checklists ensure that the evaluator considers rival explanations systematically rather than selectively.

Pre-registration of causal claims is borrowed from the experimental tradition but applies equally to theory-based methods. The evaluator states, before collecting data, what causal claims the evaluation will test and what evidence would support or undermine each claim. Pre-registration does not prevent bias, but it makes bias visible. A reader can compare the pre-registered claims with the final report and assess whether the evaluator shifted the goalposts.

Blinding separates evidence collection from causal assessment where feasible. In Bayesian process tracing, one team member can gather and code evidence while another sets the prior probabilities and conducts the updating, reducing the risk that the person interpreting the evidence is influenced by knowing what conclusion it supports. Blinding is more difficult in participatory methods, where the evaluator is often directly involved in facilitating the evidence-generation process.

Sensitivity analysis varies the analyst’s assumptions and reports whether the conclusions change. The specific form of sensitivity analysis depends on the logic being used, and each method family has developed its own protocols.

In QCA, sensitivity analysis involves testing whether the solution holds when cases are added or removed, when calibration thresholds are shifted (particularly the crossover point), or when conditions are recoded. Schneider & Wagemann (2012) recommend varying the crossover point by at least one standard deviation in each direction and reporting whether the truth table rows change and whether the minimised solution is altered. If a single case’s movement across the crossover point changes the solution, that case is pivotal, and the finding is fragile.

In Bayesian process tracing, sensitivity analysis involves varying the prior probability and the sensitivity and false-positive rate estimates for each test, and checking whether the posterior odds still support the same conclusion. Befani (2020) suggests computing posterior probabilities under “optimistic” (high sensitivity, low false-positive rate) and “pessimistic” (low sensitivity, high false-positive rate) scenarios. If the conclusion holds under both, the finding is robust to probability estimation uncertainty. If the conclusion reverses under the pessimistic scenario, the evaluation should report that the causal claim is sensitive to the analyst’s probability judgements.

In contribution analysis, sensitivity analysis involves considering alternative contribution stories and assessing whether the evidence distinguishes between them. Each causal link in the theory of change should be tested against the most plausible rival explanation for that link, not just the overall alternative hypothesis. If the contribution story rests on a single causal link for which the evidence is ambiguous, the evaluation should report that link as a point of fragility.

In SCED analysis, sensitivity analysis involves testing whether the effect estimate changes when different analytical methods (visual inspection, LMEM, NAP, Tau-U) are applied, when outlier data points are removed, or when the phase boundaries are shifted by one or two observations. Kratochwill et al. (2013) recommend that SCED studies report results under multiple analytical approaches and note whether the conclusions align.

If the conclusion is sensitive to reasonable alternative assumptions under any of these protocols, the evaluator should report that fragility rather than suppress it.

12.3 Reporting standards

Several method-specific reporting standards exist. SCRIBE (Single-Case Reporting guideline In BEhavioural interventions) provides a structured checklist for SCED studies (Tate et al., 2016). The CONSORT extension for N-of-1 trials (CENT) adapts the familiar clinical trial reporting framework to single-case designs (Shamseer et al., 2015). RAMESES (Realist And Meta-narrative Evidence Syntheses: Evolving Standards) covers realist evaluation and realist synthesis (Wong et al., 2013). Transparency standards for QCA specify how to report calibration decisions, truth table analyses, and solution terms (Schneider & Wagemann, 2010).

These standards share common elements: they require the evaluator to report design decisions, describe data sources, justify analytical choices, and acknowledge limitations. They differ in what they emphasise, reflecting the different logics on which each method rests. No existing standard covers multi-logic evaluations or addresses the specific quality challenges of combining methods across logics.

The checklist is not a replacement for method-specific standards. Evaluators using SCEDs should follow SCRIBE in addition to this checklist. Evaluators using realist evaluation should follow RAMESES. The checklist fills a gap: it provides a common framework for evaluations that combine methods, and it ensures that readers can assess the quality of a small-n evaluation regardless of which tradition it comes from.

From an EIPM perspective, structured reporting standards serve as institutional infrastructure for evidence credibility (Sempé et al., 2025). Evidence users and intermediaries in the policy ecosystem rely on the transparency and rigour of evaluation reports when deciding how much weight to give causal findings. When small-n evaluations follow ad hoc reporting conventions, the causal claims they produce are harder for non-specialist audiences to appraise, reducing the likelihood that the evidence enters the policy process. The checklist above operationalises the structures and processes pathway by providing a standardised instrument through which evaluation quality can be assessed across logics.

The final chapter draws these threads together, arguing that small-n evaluation is not a concession to constraints but a distinct mode of causal inquiry with its own standards of rigour.
