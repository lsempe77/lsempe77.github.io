6  Generative and Mechanistic Approaches

The previous chapter identified which combinations of conditions produce the outcome. It did not explain the mechanism through which those conditions operate, why the same combination succeeds in one site and fails in another, or what the causal chain looks like step by step. The generative logic addresses these questions. It warrants causal claims by identifying how the intervention produces its effects: the “cogs and wheels” (Hedström & Swedberg, 1998) that transmit the causal force from input to outcome. Pawson & Tilley (1997) formalised this as context-mechanism-outcome (CMO) configurations. A mechanism (M) fires only in the right context (C) to produce the outcome (O). The generative logic distinguishes causal explanation (tracing the mechanism) from causal description (establishing that X and Y co-occur or that a configuration is sufficient for Y).

Four method families operationalise this logic, each with different strengths. Process tracing works within a single case at high resolution. Contribution analysis draws on multiple evidence sources to build a programme-level contribution story. Realist evaluation compares CMO configurations across sites. General elimination methodology (GEM) structures the elimination of rival explanations. All four depend on a well-specified theory of change, which is discussed first.

6.1 Theory of change as a precursor

A theory of change (ToC) is not a standalone evaluation method. It is the backbone of all generative approaches. Without a ToC that specifies the causal chain from activities to outcomes, process tracing has nothing to trace, contribution analysis has no contribution story to test, and realist evaluation has no CMO configurations to compare. The distinction that matters for causal inference is between a ToC that supports testing and a logical framework that does not. A testable ToC states each causal link as a proposition that could be wrong: “teacher training improves classroom practice because trained teachers use formative assessment techniques, and formative assessment increases student engagement.” Each clause is independently verifiable. A logical framework lists inputs, outputs, and outcomes in a linear chain without specifying the causal assumptions that connect them.

Quality criteria for ToCs that support causal inference include three features. Each causal link should be stated as a testable assumption. Alternative causal pathways should be acknowledged alongside the primary pathway. Contextual conditions under which the mechanism is expected to fire should be specified. A ToC that meets these criteria provides the roadmap for process tracing (which links to test), contribution analysis (which links to gather evidence on), and realist evaluation (which CMO configurations to compare).

6.2 Process tracing

Process tracing examines the causal chain from intervention to outcome within a single case (Beach & Pedersen, 2019). The method operates at high resolution within one case rather than comparing across cases. The evaluator identifies the hypothesised mechanism (from the ToC), specifies the observable implications that the mechanism would leave if it were active, and then searches for those traces in the empirical record. If the predicted traces are found, confidence in the causal claim increases. If they are absent, confidence decreases.

Beach & Pedersen (2019) distinguish three types of process tracing, each suited to a different evaluation question. Theory-testing process tracing starts with a fully specified mechanism and tests whether it operated in the case under study. The evaluator derives predictions from the theory and checks them against evidence. Theory-building process tracing works in the opposite direction: the evaluator examines the empirical record to induce a plausible mechanism that explains the observed outcome. Explaining-outcome process tracing combines elements of both, tracing back from the outcome to construct a case-specific explanation that may draw on multiple mechanisms. Theory-testing is the most common in evaluation because evaluators usually have a ToC that specifies the expected mechanism.

The evidence pieces that process tracing examines are causal-process observations (CPOs). Each CPO carries diagnostic value: it updates the probability that the hypothesised mechanism was active. A programme document showing that the implementing agency followed the predicted decision sequence is a CPO. An interview in which a key actor describes behavioural changes consistent with the hypothesised mechanism is a CPO. The formal treatment of how CPOs update causal confidence belongs to the diagnostic logic and is covered in Chapter 7. Many practitioners combine generative and diagnostic logics in practice, using process tracing to identify the mechanism and Bayesian updating to quantify the evidential weight of each observation.

Befani & Stedman-Bryce (2017) demonstrate this combination in practice, applying Bayesian updating to process-tracing evidence in an impact evaluation, showing how each piece of evidence formally shifts the probability that the hypothesised mechanism was active.

Process tracing requires intensive within-case data: programme documents, administrative records, meeting minutes, interviews with implementers and beneficiaries, direct observation, and secondary literature. The method works at n=1, but the depth of engagement with the single case is high. In development evaluation, process tracing is well suited to tracing how a policy reform produced or failed to produce the intended change in a single country, sector, or programme site. It is common in evaluations of complex governance interventions, institutional reforms, and policy processes where the causal chain is long and context-dependent.

Piper et al. (2018) trace how Kenya’s Tusome national literacy programme scaled up successfully, identifying the specific mechanisms (coaching, materials, accountability) through which the intervention produced learning gains — a detailed process tracing of a single-country education reform.

6.3 Contribution analysis

Contribution analysis builds and iteratively strengthens a “contribution story” that explains how the intervention contributed to the observed outcome (Mayne, 2001). The method does not claim to prove attribution; it claims to establish a credible account of contribution, supported by evidence at each step of the causal chain.

Mayne’s framework proceeds in six steps. First, set out the attribution problem: what causal question needs answering, and what is the intervention’s claimed role? Second, develop or refine the theory of change, identifying the key causal links and the assumptions behind each. Third, gather existing evidence on the ToC: what is already known about whether each link holds? Fourth, assemble and assess the contribution story: given the available evidence, how strong is the claim that the intervention contributed to the outcome? Fifth, seek additional evidence where the story is weak: which links lack supporting evidence, and what new data would strengthen or weaken the claim? Sixth, revise and strengthen the contribution story in light of the new evidence. The process is iterative: steps five and six may cycle several times.

Contribution analysis differs from process tracing in scope and evidence strategy. Process tracing traces a single causal chain within one case at high resolution, relying on CPOs. Contribution analysis draws on multiple evidence sources (quantitative data, stakeholder interviews, programme records, secondary literature, monitoring data) to assess the plausibility of the entire contribution story. It is designed for programme-level evaluation rather than single-case research. The contribution story is the ToC, tested against evidence. Each causal link in the ToC is assessed for strength, and the overall claim is only as strong as the weakest link.

The distinction between strong and weak links deserves emphasis. Mayne (2012) proposes a four-level scale for assessing each causal link: not addressed (no evidence gathered), not supported (evidence does not confirm the link), plausible (some evidence supports the link but alternatives remain), and confirmed (strong evidence supports the link and alternatives have been addressed). A contribution story in which every link reaches at least the “plausible” level represents a credible contribution claim. A story with one or more links at “not supported” identifies exactly where the causal chain breaks down. This graduated assessment is what separates contribution analysis from a narrative description of the programme: each link is independently evaluated, and the overall claim is transparent about its evidential basis.

Development evaluation application: contribution analysis of a health systems programme

Dossou et al. (2020) applied contribution analysis to evaluate a performance-based financing programme in Benin. The evaluation team mapped the theory of change from financial incentives through health facility management improvements to increased service utilisation. Each causal link was assessed using facility-level data, health worker interviews, and administrative records. The analysis identified that the link between incentives and management improvements was well supported, but the link between management improvements and patient utilisation was weak in facilities with low baseline demand. Contribution analysis revealed that the programme contributed to service delivery improvements in facilities with existing patient demand but had limited impact where demand-side barriers (distance, cost to patients, cultural norms) were binding. The graduated assessment allowed the evaluation to produce a differentiated conclusion rather than a binary “worked/did not work” judgement.

When to prefer contribution analysis over process tracing depends on the evaluation question and the available evidence. Contribution analysis is the stronger choice when the evaluation covers a programme rather than a single case, when multiple evidence types are available, and when the commissioner needs a narrative that explains contribution across the full causal chain. Process tracing is the stronger choice when the question requires high-resolution tracing of a specific mechanism within one case, and when detailed within-case evidence (documents, testimony, records) is available.

6.4 Realist evaluation

Realist evaluation asks “what works, for whom, in what circumstances, and why?” (Pawson & Tilley, 1997). This question structures the entire approach. The unit of analysis is the CMO configuration: a specified context (C) triggers a specified mechanism (M) to produce a specified outcome (O). The programme does not “cause” the outcome directly; it provides resources and opportunities that activate mechanisms in specific contexts.

CMO configurations are best explained through example. A community health worker programme (the intervention) may improve maternal health outcomes (O) in rural areas with weak formal health infrastructure (C) because the programme activates trust between health workers and mothers, leading to sustained uptake of antenatal care (M). In urban areas with strong hospital access (a different C), the same programme may activate a different mechanism (supplementing rather than substituting for formal care) and produce a different outcome (small marginal improvement rather than transformative change). Realist evaluation identifies and compares these configurations rather than estimating a single average effect.

Greenhalgh et al. (2009) provide a worked example of realist evaluation applied to a health service modernisation programme in London, tracing how different contextual conditions activated different mechanisms to produce divergent outcomes across sites.

The comparison logic in realist evaluation differs from statistical comparison. The goal is not to establish whether the programme “works on average” but to identify which contexts activate which mechanisms to produce which outcomes. Case selection is purposive: the evaluator selects cases that vary on the contextual conditions hypothesised to matter, and collects data on the mechanisms hypothesised to operate. The analysis compares CMO configurations across sites, refining the programme theory with each case.

Realist synthesis extends this logic to evidence reviews. Rather than pooling effect sizes from multiple studies, realist synthesis extracts CMO configurations from each study and compares them across the evidence base. The output is a refined programme theory that specifies the conditions under which the intervention works, the mechanisms through which it works, and the outcomes it produces. Realist synthesis is well suited to reviews of small-n evaluations because it does not require standardised effect sizes or statistical homogeneity.

In development evaluation, realist evaluation is well suited to complex programmes implemented across diverse contexts: multi-country governance initiatives, community-driven development programmes, and health system strengthening efforts where the same design produces different results in different settings.

6.5 General elimination methodology

General elimination methodology (GEM) warrants causal claims by systematically eliminating rival explanations (Scriven, 2008). The logic is Popperian: if all plausible alternative explanations have been tested and ruled out, the remaining explanation is the most credible. GEM does not prove causation; it eliminates alternatives until the causal claim survives.

The practical steps require disciplined application. The evaluator begins by listing all plausible rival explanations for the observed outcome. These include standard validity threats (maturation, history, selection, regression to the mean) as well as context-specific alternatives (co-occurring interventions, changes in political leadership, external shocks, Hawthorne effects). Shadish et al. (2002) provide the canonical taxonomy of validity threats that GEM draws upon, though the list should always be extended with programme-specific rivals derived from contextual knowledge. For each rival, the evaluator specifies what observable evidence would support it and what evidence would contradict it. Evidence is then gathered to test each rival. Rivals that the evidence contradicts are eliminated. Rivals that the evidence cannot disconfirm remain as standing alternatives that qualify the causal conclusion.

The strength of the remaining causal claim depends on three factors: how many rivals were considered, how rigorously each was tested, and how plausible the surviving alternatives are. An evaluation that considers only two or three obvious rivals and fails to test context-specific alternatives provides a weaker warrant than one that conducts a systematic search. GEM is often used alongside process tracing or contribution analysis to structure the elimination of rival hypotheses rather than as a standalone design. Its value is as an inferential discipline: it forces the evaluator to consider and test alternatives rather than building a case for the preferred explanation alone. Befani (2012) note that GEM complements Bayesian process tracing: the rivals that GEM identifies become the alternative hypotheses against which the diagnostic tests in Chapter 7 are calibrated.

Development evaluation application: GEM in a governance reform evaluation

Palenberg (2011) applied a structured rival-elimination approach in an evaluation of governance support programmes in sub-Saharan Africa. The evaluation team identified seven plausible rival explanations for observed improvements in public financial management (including donor coordination effects, leadership transitions, and parallel reform programmes funded by other agencies). Each rival was tested against documentary evidence, key informant interviews, and administrative data. Four rivals were eliminated; three survived as partial alternative explanations. The final causal claim was that the programme contributed to the observed improvements in conjunction with favourable leadership conditions, a more qualified and credible conclusion than an unstructured assessment would have produced.

6.6 Strengths, limitations, and applicability

Table 6.1 summarises the conditions under which each generative method is applicable.

The principal strengths of generative methods are well matched to the questions that development evaluators face most often. They provide causal explanation: not just whether the intervention worked, but how and why it worked. They work at n=1 (process tracing) or small n (realist evaluation). They are closely aligned with the theory-based evaluation tradition that most development evaluators already practise. They address “how” and “why” questions that counterfactual and configurational methods cannot answer.

The limitations are important. Generative methods are labour-intensive. Process tracing requires deep engagement with a single case: weeks of document review, multiple rounds of interviews, and iterative analysis. Contribution analysis requires multiple evidence sources and several iterative cycles. Realist evaluation requires data collection on contexts and mechanisms across multiple sites, not just on outcomes. Generalisability depends on replication: a process tracing study of one country cannot, on its own, tell the evaluator whether the mechanism operates in other countries. Mechanism identification carries a risk of subjectivity: two analysts examining the same evidence may construct different mechanistic accounts, and the method provides no formal procedure for resolving the disagreement.

The case against and for generative methods

Three criticisms recur in the methodological literature. First, mechanism claims are difficult to falsify because the mechanism is not directly observed; the evaluator infers its presence from observable traces, and the inference depends on the evaluator’s theoretical framework. Second, theory-based approaches risk confirmation bias: the ToC specifies what evidence to seek, and the evaluator may attend selectively to evidence that confirms the hypothesised mechanism while neglecting disconfirming evidence. Third, contribution analysis and realist evaluation can become tautological if the ToC is not independently testable: the programme theory explains the outcome, and the outcome validates the programme theory.

Proponents respond on each point. On falsifiability, process tracing generates specific predictions that can fail. A theory-testing process trace that predicts five observable implications and finds only two has produced a negative result; the method is falsifiable at the level of individual tests. On confirmation bias, the iterative design of contribution analysis explicitly requires the evaluator to seek additional evidence where the story is weak, and GEM’s rival-elimination logic forces attention to alternative explanations. On tautology, realist evaluation compares CMO configurations across sites: if the hypothesised mechanism fires only in some contexts and not others, the cross-site comparison provides a test that a single-site study cannot. The diagnostic logic covered in the next chapter adds a further layer of rigour by quantifying how much each piece of evidence should shift confidence in the causal claim.

The generative logic identifies the mechanism, but it does not formally quantify how much each piece of evidence should change confidence in the causal claim. The next chapter provides that formal machinery through the diagnostic logic and Bayesian updating.
