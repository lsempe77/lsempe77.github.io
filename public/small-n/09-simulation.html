9  Computational Simulation as Complement to Small-n Designs

Agent-based modelling and system dynamics are not themselves small-n evaluation methods. They are modelling frameworks that operate at any scale, from a handful of agents to millions. This chapter positions them as complementary tools that strengthen small-n evaluations by filling three specific gaps: generating counterfactual scenarios when empirical comparators are unavailable, estimating probabilities needed for diagnostic evaluation, and exploring the emergent dynamics of complex interventions. The chapter concentrates on how simulation supports causal inference in small-n contexts, rather than on computational technique. Readers seeking programming guidance should consult Gilbert & Troitzsch (2005) for agent-based modelling and Sterman (2000) for system dynamics.

9.1 Agent-based modelling

An agent-based model (ABM) represents a system as a collection of autonomous agents that follow behavioural rules. Each agent perceives its local environment, interacts with neighbouring agents, and updates its state accordingly. The aggregate behaviour of the system emerges from these micro-level interactions rather than from a set of top-down equations. This bottom-up architecture makes ABM well suited to modelling causal mechanisms, because the analyst must specify the rules that produce the outcome rather than merely estimating a relationship between variables.

Three features of ABM are relevant for small-n evaluation. First, ABM can generate the probability estimates that Bayesian process tracing requires. Befani et al. (2021) demonstrated this approach by building a simulation of the causal mechanism under evaluation, running it thousands of times with different parameter values, and using the resulting distributions as prior and likelihood estimates. This “third way” sits alongside empirical frequency data and subjective expert elicitation as a source of probability estimates (Chapter 7). When empirical frequency data are unavailable and expert elicitation is contested, simulation offers a transparent and replicable alternative. Second, ABM can model the mechanism chains that process tracing investigates. A theory of change posits that a training programme improves teacher practice, which improves student outcomes. An ABM can represent teachers as agents with varying skill levels, model the training intervention as a rule change, and observe whether the simulated mechanism chain produces the predicted pattern. If the ABM reproduces the observed outcome only under specific parametric assumptions, that constrains the set of plausible causal accounts. Third, ABM can construct virtual counterfactuals for cases where no empirical comparison group exists. A one-off policy reform in a single country cannot be compared with a control country, but an ABM calibrated to the pre-reform period can simulate the trajectory that would have occurred without the reform.

Maroulis et al. (2010) use an ABM of school choice in Chicago to show how agent interactions produce emergent enrolment patterns that a regression model would miss — a published example of ABM applied to education policy evaluation. The ABM tradition traces to Epstein & Axtell (1996), whose Growing Artificial Societies demonstrated how complex social outcomes emerge from simple agent rules.

Validation is the central challenge. An ABM is only as credible as its rules, and the analyst must justify those rules with empirical evidence or well-established theory. Pattern-oriented modelling offers one approach: the analyst specifies multiple empirical patterns that the model must reproduce simultaneously, which constrains the parameter space and reduces the risk of overfitting to a single outcome. Even so, simulation results should be treated as structured thought experiments rather than as empirical findings. They sharpen causal reasoning; they do not replace empirical evidence.

9.2 System dynamics

System dynamics (SD) models represent a system as a set of stocks (accumulations), flows (rates of change), and feedback loops. A causal loop diagram maps the qualitative structure: which variables reinforce each other (positive feedback) and which counteract each other (negative feedback). A quantified SD model translates this diagram into differential equations that can be simulated over time.

SD supports small-n evaluation in two ways. First, it provides a formal framework for mapping and testing a theory of change in feedback-rich systems. Many development interventions operate in systems where the outcome feeds back into the conditions that produced it. A microfinance programme increases household income, which increases repayment rates, which increases the loan fund, which enables more lending. An SD model makes these feedback structures explicit and testable. If the theory of change assumes a reinforcing loop but the SD model shows that a balancing loop (such as market saturation) dominates after a threshold, the evaluator has identified a structural flaw in the programme logic before collecting a single additional data point.

Second, SD enables scenario analysis for contribution analysis (Chapter 6). The central question in contribution analysis is whether the programme made a difference given all the other factors at play. An SD model can simulate two scenarios: one with the programme and one without. The difference between the two trajectories represents the programme’s simulated contribution. This is not a counterfactual in the experimental sense, because it depends on model assumptions. It is a structured counterfactual whose assumptions are transparent and open to scrutiny. Sterman (2000) provides the foundational treatment of SD modelling, including calibration, sensitivity testing, and validation procedures.

Development evaluation application: system dynamics for health system capacity planning

Homer & Hirsch (2006) developed a system dynamics model of the tuberculosis epidemic in India, modelling the feedback loops between detection, treatment, drug resistance, and transmission. The model was calibrated using epidemiological data from national health surveys. The evaluation team simulated two scenarios: one with an expanded DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) programme and one without. The difference between the two trajectories estimated the programme’s contribution to case detection and mortality reduction over a 20-year horizon. The model identified a counterintuitive finding: expanding detection without adequate treatment capacity would increase drug-resistant TB through incomplete treatment courses, a negative feedback loop that verbal programme logic had not anticipated. The SD model disciplined the contribution analysis by revealing structural dynamics that cross-sectional comparison could not detect.

9.3 Integration with small-n methods

The value of simulation for small-n evaluation lies in its integration with the methods covered in earlier chapters. Table 9.1 summarises three concrete pairings.

The first pairing is the most developed in the literature. Befani et al. (2021) showed that an ABM of a health intervention could generate the sensitivity and specificity estimates needed for diagnostic tests, addressing the probability estimation challenge discussed in Chapter 7. The second pairing remains more conceptual. Several authors have used SD to map theories of change, but few have formally linked the SD output to a contribution analysis framework. The potential is clear: SD makes the contribution story dynamic and testable, but the integration protocol needs further methodological development. The third pairing is exploratory. QCA results depend on the cases included in the analysis, and an ABM can generate synthetic cases that test whether the solution holds under different parameter assumptions. This is analogous to a robustness check in statistical analysis, but implemented through simulation rather than through alternative model specifications.

9.4 Strengths, limitations, and applicability

Simulation handles complexity, non-linearity, and emergence in ways that verbal theories of change cannot. An ABM can reveal unintended consequences that arise from agent interactions. An SD model can identify tipping points and feedback traps. Both force the evaluator to make causal assumptions explicit, which disciplines the reasoning process even when the model itself is imperfect.

The limitations are equally clear. Model validity is always contestable. Every simulation rests on assumptions about agent rules (ABM) or functional forms (SD), and different assumptions can produce different conclusions. The risk of confirmation bias applies: an analyst who believes in the programme’s effectiveness may unconsciously calibrate the model to confirm that belief. Computational expertise is a practical barrier. Building and validating a simulation model requires skills that most evaluation teams do not possess, and the cost of acquiring those skills may not be justified for every evaluation. Finally, simulation results are model-dependent, not data-dependent. They complement empirical evidence; they do not substitute for it.

The case against and for computational simulation

Three criticisms recur. First, simulation models can produce any result by adjusting their assumptions. A model is only as good as its rules, and with enough degrees of freedom, the analyst can fit any narrative. Second, simulation adds a layer of abstraction that distances the evaluation from the empirical evidence. Stakeholders may distrust findings that rest on “what-if” scenarios rather than on observed data. Third, the expertise and time required to build credible simulations may not be proportionate to the evaluation budget, especially in resource-constrained settings.

Proponents respond on each point. On assumption sensitivity, pattern-oriented modelling and systematic sensitivity analysis constrain the parameter space and make the analyst’s choices transparent. A well-documented simulation is more transparent than a verbal theory of change, because every assumption is explicit and testable. On abstraction, simulation does not replace empirical evidence. It structures the evaluator’s reasoning about how the evidence fits together, and it fills specific gaps (probability estimates, counterfactual scenarios) that empirical methods cannot address in small-n settings. On expertise, the barrier is real but declining. User-friendly platforms (NetLogo for ABM, Vensim for SD) have lowered the technical threshold, and the growing availability of training resources is expanding the pool of evaluators who can use these tools competently.
