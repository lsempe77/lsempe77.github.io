7  Diagnostic Approaches

The previous chapter showed how generative methods identify the mechanism connecting intervention to outcome. Process tracing, in particular, collects causal-process observations and uses them to update confidence in the hypothesised causal chain. But the generative logic does not specify how much each piece of evidence should change that confidence. Two evaluators examining the same CPO may reach different conclusions about its evidential weight. The diagnostic logic provides the formal machinery for resolving this problem. It treats each piece of evidence as a diagnostic test and uses Bayesian updating to quantify how much the evidence shifts the probability that the causal claim is true.

The diagnostic logic is not a rival to the generative logic; it is its inferential engine. Process tracing identifies what to look for. Bayesian updating determines how much what is found should matter. The combination of the two produces Bayesian process tracing, the method that occupies most of this chapter. The chapter also covers the practical challenges of estimating the probabilities that Bayesian updating requires, and a recent innovation that uses computational simulation to generate those estimates when empirical frequencies and subjective elicitation are both infeasible.

7.1 The diagnostic logic

The diagnostic logic evaluates causal claims through the probative value of evidence. Each piece of evidence is treated as a diagnostic test with two properties: sensitivity (the probability of observing the evidence if the causal claim is true) and the false-positive rate (the probability of observing the evidence if the causal claim is false). These two properties determine the likelihood ratio, which measures the evidential weight of the observation.

The Bayesian machinery proceeds in three steps. The evaluator begins with prior odds that the causal claim is true, based on available background knowledge. The evaluator then observes a piece of evidence and computes its likelihood ratio: sensitivity divided by the false-positive rate. The prior odds are multiplied by the likelihood ratio to produce posterior odds. If a second piece of evidence is observed, the posterior odds from the first update become the prior odds for the second, and the process repeats. The result is a transparent, sequential updating of causal confidence as evidence accumulates.

The updating rule can be stated formally. Let \(H\) denote the hypothesis that the intervention caused the outcome, and let \(\neg H\) denote the alternative (the outcome would have occurred without the intervention). The prior odds are \(O(H) = P(H) / P(\neg H)\). For a piece of evidence \(E\), the likelihood ratio is:

\[ LR = \frac{P(E \mid H)}{P(E \mid \neg H)} \tag{7.1}\]

where \(P(E \mid H)\) is the probability of observing the evidence if the hypothesis is true (sensitivity) and \(P(E \mid \neg H)\) is the probability of observing the evidence if the hypothesis is false (the false-positive rate). The posterior odds after observing \(E\) are:

\[ O(H \mid E) = O(H) \times LR \tag{7.2}\]

When multiple independent pieces of evidence \(E_1, E_2, \ldots, E_k\) are observed, the posterior odds are the product of the prior odds and all likelihood ratios: \(O(H \mid E_1, \ldots, E_k) = O(H) \times LR_1 \times LR_2 \times \cdots \times LR_k\). The posterior probability can be recovered from the posterior odds as \(P(H \mid E) = O(H \mid E) / (1 + O(H \mid E))\).

The relationship to process tracing is direct. The causal-process observations that process tracing collects are the diagnostic tests. A programme document confirming that the implementing agency followed the predicted decision sequence is a test with known sensitivity and false-positive rate. An interview in which a key actor describes behavioural changes consistent with the hypothesis is another test. The diagnostic logic provides the formal framework for combining these tests into an overall assessment of the causal claim.

7.2 Bayesian process tracing in practice

The political science literature formalised four classic process-tracing tests, each defined by its combination of sensitivity and specificity (Collier, 2011). The straw-in-the-wind test has low sensitivity and low specificity: the evidence is consistent with the hypothesis but also consistent with alternatives. Passing the test slightly increases confidence; failing it slightly decreases confidence. The hoop test has high sensitivity but low specificity: the evidence is expected if the hypothesis is true (high sensitivity), but it is also expected under many alternatives (low specificity). Failing a hoop test strongly undermines the hypothesis; passing it is only mildly confirmatory. The smoking-gun test has low sensitivity but high specificity: the evidence is unlikely to be observed (low sensitivity) but, if observed, is strongly confirmatory because it is very unlikely under alternatives (high specificity). The doubly decisive test has both high sensitivity and high specificity: the evidence is expected if the hypothesis is true and unexpected if it is false. A single doubly decisive test can confirm the hypothesis on its own.

In practice, the evaluator maps each CPO to one of these test types, assigns approximate sensitivity and false-positive rate values, and computes the likelihood ratio. The updating is iterative: evidence accumulates across multiple tests, and the posterior odds after the final test represent the overall confidence in the causal claim. The sequence in which evidence is considered does not affect the final posterior odds, provided the tests are independent. Independence is a strong assumption, and its violations are a practical challenge discussed below.

A worked example illustrates the mechanics. Suppose an evaluator assesses whether a parliamentary strengthening programme caused improved legislative oversight in a single country. The prior probability is set at 0.5 (agnostic), giving prior odds of 1:1. Three pieces of evidence are collected.

First, a hoop test: parliamentary records show that oversight committees met regularly after the programme began. This evidence is expected if the hypothesis is true (sensitivity = 0.90) but also expected under alternatives such as a concurrent governance reform (false-positive rate = 0.60). The likelihood ratio is \(0.90/0.60 = 1.5\). Posterior odds after the first test: \(1.0 \times 1.5 = 1.5\), corresponding to a posterior probability of 0.60.

Second, a smoking-gun test: internal government documents show that committee members explicitly referenced programme training materials when designing new oversight procedures. This evidence is less likely to be observed (sensitivity = 0.40) but very unlikely under the alternative (false-positive rate = 0.05). The likelihood ratio is \(0.40/0.05 = 8.0\). Posterior odds after the second test: \(1.5 \times 8.0 = 12.0\), corresponding to a posterior probability of 0.92.

Third, a straw-in-the-wind test: government officials express positive views about the programme in interviews. This evidence is somewhat expected under the hypothesis (sensitivity = 0.70) and also somewhat expected under alternatives, given social desirability (false-positive rate = 0.50). The likelihood ratio is \(0.70/0.50 = 1.4\). Posterior odds after the third test: \(12.0 \times 1.4 = 16.8\), corresponding to a posterior probability of 0.94.

The final posterior probability of 0.94 reflects moderate-to-strong confidence in the causal claim. The updating trail is fully transparent: a reader who disagrees with any probability estimate can substitute an alternative value and recompute. The smoking-gun evidence (the internal documents) contributed the most inferential weight, as one would expect from its high likelihood ratio.

Development evaluation application: Bayesian process tracing of policy influence

Befani & Stedman-Bryce (2017) applied Bayesian process tracing to evaluate the impact of a research programme on policy change in the health sector. The evaluation team identified observable implications of the hypothesised influence pathway (uptake of research findings in policy documents, citation in parliamentary debates, changes in budget allocations consistent with recommendations). Each piece of evidence was classified as a hoop, smoking-gun, or straw-in-the-wind test, and sensitivity and false-positive rate values were elicited from the evaluation team using structured protocols. The sequential updating produced a posterior probability that the research programme had contributed to the observed policy change, with the strongest evidential weight coming from budget allocation changes that mirrored the programme’s specific recommendations. The worked example demonstrated that Bayesian process tracing can produce formal, auditable causal conclusions for single-case policy evaluations.

7.3 Practical challenges and solutions

Befani (2020) identifies three practical challenges that arise when applying Bayesian updating to theory-based evaluation. The first is probability estimation. The Bayesian machinery requires numerical values for sensitivity and the false-positive rate, but evaluators rarely have empirical frequency data for the causal mechanisms under study. How often does a programme document confirming the predicted decision sequence appear when the hypothesis is true? How often does it appear when the hypothesis is false? These frequencies are seldom known. Befani (2020) proposes two solutions: using qualitative confidence descriptors (“very likely,” “unlikely”) anchored to numerical ranges, and using structured elicitation protocols in which the evaluation team collectively estimates the probabilities.

The second challenge is confirmation bias and conservatism. Evaluation teams that have invested in a theory of change may assign overly high sensitivity values to confirming evidence (they expect to find it because they believe the hypothesis) and overly low false-positive rates (they underestimate the chance of finding it under alternatives). Befani (2020) recommends explicit “red team” exercises in which a team member argues the rival hypothesis and re-estimates the probabilities from that perspective. Conservatism, the opposite problem, arises when evaluators are reluctant to assign extreme probability values, compressing likelihood ratios toward 1 and producing updates that are too small. The solution is to anchor probability estimates in concrete counterfactual reasoning: “if the programme had not been implemented, how likely is it that this document would exist?”

The third challenge is evidence packages. Bayesian updating assumes that each test is independent: the result of one test does not predict the result of another. In practice, evidence often comes in bundles. Interview testimony from three programme staff members may all reflect the same institutional narrative rather than providing three independent observations. Befani (2020) proposes setting boundaries on independent observations by asking whether each piece of evidence could, in principle, turn out differently from the others. Evidence that cannot vary independently should be treated as a single observation with a single likelihood ratio, not as multiple independent tests.

7.4 Simulated probabilities for diagnostic evaluation

When empirical frequency data are unavailable and subjective elicitation is difficult, Befani et al. (2021) propose a third approach: using computational simulation to generate the probability estimates that Bayesian updating requires. The method works as follows. The evaluation team builds an agent-based model that encodes the hypothesised causal mechanism. The model is run under two conditions: one in which the mechanism is active (the hypothesis is true) and one in which it is not (the hypothesis is false). For each condition, the model generates the expected pattern of observable evidence. The proportion of simulation runs that produce the observed evidence pattern under the “true” condition estimates sensitivity. The proportion that produce it under the “false” condition estimates the false-positive rate.

Befani et al. (2021) demonstrate the approach with a health policy application, showing that simulation-derived probability estimates produce posterior odds that are consistent with expert judgement but are more transparent and reproducible. The approach is warranted when three conditions hold: the causal mechanism is well enough understood to build a plausible simulation model, empirical frequency data do not exist, and the evaluation team lacks the domain expertise for confident subjective elicitation. Computational simulation as a broader tool for small-n evaluation is discussed in Chapter 9.

7.5 Strengths, limitations, and applicability

Table 7.2 summarises the diagnostic methods covered in this chapter.

The principal strengths of the diagnostic logic are formality and transparency. The updating process is explicit: prior odds, likelihood ratios, and posterior odds are all stated and can be inspected, challenged, and revised. The framework is cumulative: new evidence can be incorporated as it becomes available, and the sequence of updates creates a transparent audit trail. The diagnostic logic is the only logic in this book that formally quantifies uncertainty about the causal claim.

The limitations centre on the probability estimation bottleneck. Sensitivity and false-positive rate values are rarely available from empirical data. Subjective elicitation can produce values that reflect the evaluator’s priors rather than genuine evidential weight. The independence assumption is frequently violated in practice, and violations inflate the apparent strength of the evidence. The learning curve is steep: evaluators trained in qualitative or theory-based traditions may find the Bayesian formalism unfamiliar. Befani (2020) notes that these are implementation challenges, not fundamental objections: the diagnostic logic is sound; the difficulty lies in applying it well.

The case against and for diagnostic evaluation

Two criticisms recur. First, the probabilities are “made up”: in the absence of empirical frequency data, sensitivity and false-positive rates are subjective estimates, and the Bayesian formalism lends a false precision to what is ultimately a qualitative judgement. Second, the method is too complex for routine evaluation practice: the Bayesian machinery requires training that most evaluation teams do not have, and the probability estimation step adds substantial time to the evaluation.

Proponents respond that all causal inference involves implicit probability assessment. When a process-tracing analyst concludes that a smoking-gun piece of evidence “strongly confirms” the hypothesis, the analyst is making an implicit claim about its likelihood ratio. The Bayesian framework makes that claim explicit and challengeable, which is an improvement over implicit reasoning even when the numerical estimates are imprecise. On complexity, Befani (2020) demonstrates that structured elicitation with qualitative descriptors (“very likely,” “unlikely”) reduces the technical burden without abandoning the formal structure. The simulated probability approach of Befani et al. (2021) further reduces reliance on subjective judgement. The diagnostic logic is demanding, but its demands are the price of making evidential reasoning transparent.
