2  Defining Small-n

“Small n” is not a single condition. The concept used in the evaluation literature covers at least four distinct situations, each posing different constraints on causal inference and calling for different methodological responses. White & Phillips (2012) introduced a typology of small-n settings; the present chapter refines and extends it, drawing on the subsequent literature and on the practical realities of development evaluation.

The first situation involves few programme units. A governance reform may target a single ministry. A decentralisation programme may operate across three provinces. A health system strengthening intervention may cover five districts within one region. In each case, the unit that received the intervention exists in small numbers, and the evaluator cannot increase the sample by changing the design. The indicative range is one to five treated units, though the precise threshold depends on what the evaluator needs to estimate.

Andrews (2013) documents public financial management reforms that targeted single ministries across several countries, illustrating the “few programme units” condition. Muralidharan et al. (2016) evaluate a biometric smartcard roll-out across five districts in Andhra Pradesh, India — a canonical case of a district-level intervention with very few treated units.

The second situation involves few participants within a unit. A judicial capacity-building programme may train 15 judges. A leadership development initiative may enrol 20 school headteachers. A specialised agricultural extension scheme may work with 25 farmers selected for specific agroecological conditions. The programme reaches a bounded population, sometimes a census, and the evaluator works with all of them rather than sampling from a larger pool. Indicative thresholds vary by method, but most standard statistical techniques lose reliability below 30 observations, and many development programmes operate well below this level.

Bloom et al. (2015) study management practices in a small number of schools, working with headteachers as the units of analysis. Davis et al. (2012) evaluate Farmer Field Schools in East Africa with small farmer cohorts per site — a typical “few participants” setting.

The third situation involves thin comparison groups. A quasi-experimental design may require matched comparators, but only 12 districts share the relevant baseline characteristics. A regression discontinuity design may have few observations near the threshold. A difference-in-differences estimator may have adequate treated units but struggle to identify untreated units with parallel pre-treatment trends. In these cases, the treated sample may not itself be small, but the effective sample for causal identification is.

The fourth situation involves few time points. Annual outcome data may span only three or four waves. A monitoring system may have been introduced two years before the evaluation. Seasonal data collection may yield six observations. Methods that rely on temporal variation, such as interrupted time series, require long panels to separate intervention effects from secular trends. When the temporal dimension is short, even a single-unit design faces additional constraints beyond the cross-sectional sample size.

These four dimensions are independent. A programme may be small on one dimension and adequate on another. A national reform (one treated unit) may generate rich within-case data from hundreds of administrative records. A training programme with 20 participants may have 50 repeated measurements per person. The methodological implications differ accordingly, and the choice of evaluation method depends on which dimension is constrained.

The small-n condition is not distributed randomly across the development portfolio. It clusters in precisely the settings where the demand for evidence is highest and the capacity to produce it is weakest. Fragile and conflict-affected states, institutional reform programmes, and policy pilot interventions all combine small-n constraints with the political instability, resource limitations, and weak institutional capacity that the EIPM framework identifies as moderating factors in the evidence-to-policy process (Sempé et al., 2025). When these moderating factors coincide with the absence of appropriate evaluation methods, the result is a compounding gap: evidence is hardest to generate in the contexts where it is hardest to use. Recognising which dimension of small n is binding is therefore not only a methodological exercise but a prerequisite for producing evidence that can enter the policy ecosystem at all.

The statistical consequences of small n are well established. Statistical power declines as the sample shrinks, and the probability of detecting a real effect falls to levels that make null results uninformative. Asymptotic properties that justify standard test statistics and confidence intervals cease to hold. The sampling distribution of the test statistic cannot be reliably estimated, so conventional significance thresholds (p < 0.05) lose their intended meaning (White & Phillips, 2012). The distinction between design-based and model-based inference becomes consequential: design-based inference, which derives its validity from randomisation rather than from distributional assumptions, can remain valid at very small n if the randomisation structure is known (see Chapter 4 on randomisation inference). Model-based inference, by contrast, depends on parametric assumptions that are difficult to verify with few observations.

These statistical limitations constrain what can be detected, not what exists. The insight is older than modern statistics. Aristotle held that causation is a real feature of the natural world: things have causal powers by virtue of what they are, and understanding a phenomenon means identifying the causes that produced it (Aristotle, 1930). This realist view prevailed for two millennia, until Hume (1748) argued that we never observe causation directly but only regular conjunction between events, and that causal claims are therefore always inferences from repeated experience. Much of modern statistical methodology inherits the Humean framework: causal effects are defined as differences across many observations, and small samples are treated as obstacles to causal knowledge because they provide too few conjunctions to ground a reliable inference. Bhaskar (2008) recovered the Aristotelian position within a modern philosophy of science, arguing that causal powers are real properties of structures and mechanisms, whether or not anyone observes them. Cartwright (2007) argued that no single method holds a monopoly on causal knowledge, and that different inferential strategies capture different aspects of the causal relationship. Pawson & Tilley (1997) grounded this insight in evaluation practice, showing that causal mechanisms operate in the real world and that the evaluator’s task is to uncover them, not to create them through design. Recognising the distinction between the existence of a causal effect and the ability to demonstrate it through a particular statistical framework is essential, because it opens the door to alternative modes of causal reasoning rather than treating small n as a dead end.

When n is small, each case carries rich, detailed information. A single ministry undergoing reform generates documents, key informant perspectives, process data, and contextual knowledge that no large-n study could gather across hundreds of units. This density of within-case evidence creates opportunities for causal inference strategies that do not depend on cross-case comparison at all.

Collins et al. (2009) demonstrate the value of within-case depth by tracking the financial lives of individual poor households over a year, generating causal insight from detailed longitudinal case studies rather than cross-sectional averages.

Holland (1986) distinguished between “effects of causes” (what is the average effect of treatment X?) and “causes of effects” (what caused outcome Y in this particular case?). Large-n methods are designed for the first question. Small-n methods can address both, and some exist precisely for the second (Fox et al., 2022). The two questions are complementary, and an evaluator facing a small-n setting is not limited to a weaker form of inquiry; rather, the form of inquiry changes in kind.

2.1 Boundary methods

Several evaluation methods appear to operate at small n on one dimension but require large n on another. These boundary cases help clarify the scope of this paper.

Synthetic control methods reconstruct a counterfactual for a single treated unit by constructing a weighted combination of untreated units (Abadie, 2021). The treated unit is n = 1, but the method requires a donor pool of 20 to 50 or more untreated units observed over a long pre-treatment panel. The synthetic control method solves a “few treated units” problem, not a “few total units” problem. It is an important method for policy evaluation, but its data requirements place it outside the core scope of the small-n toolkit as defined here.

The founding application is Abadie & Gardeazabal (2003), who estimate the economic costs of the Basque Country conflict using a synthetic control constructed from other Spanish regions — a single treated unit with a donor pool of comparators.

Econometric time-series methods such as interrupted time series and ARIMA models can analyse a single unit, but they substitute temporal n for cross-sectional n. A reliable interrupted time series analysis typically requires 50 or more time points to model the counterfactual trend with confidence. When the temporal dimension is short, these methods face the same fundamental constraints as cross-sectional designs with few units.

Microsimulation models individual-level transitions through policy rules and can operate at the level of a single policy reform. However, the method typically requires large representative micro-datasets to populate the simulation. The unit of policy interest may be singular, but the data demands are large-n in character.

These boundary cases are not excluded because they lack rigour. They are treated separately because the constraints they address, and the data they require, differ from the situations described in Table 2.1. Table 2.2 summarises the distinction.

2.2 Constraint and choice

Small-n evaluation arises in two distinct ways, and the distinction matters for how evaluators and commissioners think about method selection.

In many cases, small n is a constraint. The programme was implemented in three districts. The training reached 12 participants. The outcome data span four years. No design decision can change these facts, and the evaluator must select methods that produce valid inferences within the available sample. This is the situation most evaluation guidance addresses, and it is the most common entry point for the methods described in this paper.

In other cases, small n is a deliberate choice. The evaluator could, in principle, conduct a large-n study, but the evaluation question requires a different form of evidence. A commissioner who needs to understand how a reform produced its effects in a specific institutional context will learn more from a within-case process tracing study than from a cross-site average treatment effect. An evaluation of a complex programme operating through multiple causal pathways may require configurational analysis to identify which combinations of conditions produced the outcome, a question that averaging across cases would obscure. A programme intended for scale-up may need explanatory evidence about context-mechanism-outcome interactions, not just an effect size that may not transfer.

The implication is that small-n methods are not a second-best option reserved for situations where large-n designs are infeasible. They are a first-choice option when the evaluation question demands causal explanation, configurational analysis, or within-case depth. The decision framework in Chapter 10 formalises this reasoning.
