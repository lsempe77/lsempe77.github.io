8  Participatory and Agency-Based Approaches

The methods covered so far treat the evaluator as the epistemic authority. The evaluator designs the study, specifies the causal hypothesis, collects the data, and warrants the causal claim. The participatory logic shifts part of that authority to programme participants and stakeholders. Beneficiaries have privileged access to information that no external evaluator can observe directly: whether their behaviour changed because of the programme, what alternative explanations they consider plausible, and what unexpected changes occurred. The participatory logic uses this access as a source of causal evidence, not merely as a perspective to be documented.

An important distinction must be stated at the outset. Participatory methods do not establish cause and effect in the same sense as counterfactual, configurational, or diagnostic designs. They produce stakeholder-validated contribution claims: participants report what changed, attribute the change to the programme, and independent verification assesses whether the attribution is plausible. The causal warrant is “those who experienced the change attribute it to the intervention, and independent reviewers find the claim credible.” That warrant is weaker on confounding than a randomised design or a Bayesian process trace, because no formal mechanism rules out alternative explanations. The strength of participatory methods lies elsewhere: they capture causal knowledge that external observation cannot access, and they surface unexpected outcomes that pre-specified indicators would miss. They are strongest when combined with other logics, a point this chapter returns to at the end.

A second distinction matters. “Participatory methods” is a broad term covering everything from participatory rural appraisal to focus group discussions. This chapter is not about participatory methods in general. It covers two specific methods that use stakeholder knowledge to support contribution claims: Most Significant Change (MSC) and outcome harvesting. Both include structured verification procedures that go beyond recording what participants say.

8.1 Most Significant Change

Most Significant Change is a story-based method that collects change narratives from programme participants and stakeholders, subjects them to structured selection, and verifies the selected narratives against independent evidence (Davies & Dart, 2005). The method proceeds in three steps.

The first step is story collection. Programme participants and field staff are asked an open-ended question: “what was the most significant change that occurred as a result of the programme?” The question is deliberately open. It does not specify which outcome domain to report on, and it invites respondents to describe changes that the evaluator may not have anticipated. Each story includes a description of the change and the respondent’s explanation of why the programme contributed to it.

The second step is systematic selection. Panels at multiple levels of the programme (field offices, regional offices, headquarters) review the collected stories and select those they judge most significant. Each panel records its selection rationale: why this story, and not others, represents the most important change. The selection process surfaces the values and priorities of different stakeholder groups, which is itself evaluative information.

The third step is verification. Selected stories are cross-checked against programme records, third-party observation, or follow-up interviews. Verification transforms the story from testimony into evidence. An unverified story is a stakeholder perception; a verified story is a contribution claim supported by corroboration. The strength of the causal warrant depends on the rigour of this step. Effective verification involves at least three components: triangulation against documentary sources (programme records, administrative data, media reports), follow-up interviews with people named in or affected by the reported change, and assessment by reviewers who were not involved in the original story collection. Davies & Dart (2005) emphasise that verification is not a binary judgement (true or false) but a graduated assessment of plausibility, specificity, and corroboration strength.

MSC does not estimate an effect size. It does not claim to rule out confounding. What it produces is a curated set of verified change narratives in which the programme’s contribution has been assessed by those closest to the change and corroborated by independent evidence. The method captures unexpected and emergent outcomes that pre-specified indicators would miss. It works at very small n, including a single programme site. It surfaces the outcomes that matter most to participants rather than to the evaluator or the commissioner.

Development evaluation application: MSC in a community development programme

Willetts & Crawford (2007) applied MSC to evaluate a community water and sanitation programme in Vanuatu. Story collection yielded narratives from community members describing changes ranging from improved health outcomes to shifts in gender dynamics around water-point management. Selection panels at local, provincial, and national levels identified the most significant changes and recorded their selection rationale. Verification included follow-up visits to story locations, cross-checking with health clinic records, and interviews with community leaders not involved in the programme. The MSC process revealed that the most significant change from the community’s perspective was not the health improvement (which the programme had targeted) but the empowerment of women who gained management roles at water points, an outcome that pre-specified indicators had not anticipated.

The limitations are equally clear. Not all participants submit stories, and those who do may be systematically different from those who do not: more engaged, more literate, or more favourably disposed toward the programme. The selection panels’ criteria may reflect organisational priorities rather than evidential weight. Aggregation across stories is difficult because each story is unique and context-specific. The method does not address confounding: if a concurrent policy change also affected the participants, MSC provides no formal way to separate the programme’s contribution from the policy’s contribution. In development evaluation, MSC is widely used in NGO and civil-society evaluations of complex programmes with diffuse or hard-to-predict outcomes, including community development, empowerment, and governance interventions.

8.2 Outcome harvesting

Outcome harvesting reverses the direction of evaluation inquiry (Wilson-Grau & Britt, 2012). Rather than starting from a theory of change and testing whether predicted outcomes occurred, outcome harvesting starts from observed outcomes and traces them back to the intervention’s contribution. The direction is harvest first, attribute second.

The six-step procedure proceeds as follows. First, design the harvest: define the scope, intended users, and available resources. Second, gather data and draft outcome descriptions: programme staff and partners identify changes they have observed and describe the intervention’s claimed contribution to each. Third, engage change agents: those who were directly involved review and refine the outcome descriptions, correcting errors and adding detail. Fourth, substantiate: independent reviewers verify each outcome against documentary and third-party evidence. Fifth, analyse and interpret: look for patterns across the harvested outcomes. Sixth, support use of findings: present results to intended users.

Substantiation is the critical step for causal credibility. Each harvested outcome is reviewed by someone independent of the programme, who assesses whether the evidence supports the claimed contribution. Substantiation typically involves three procedures. First, the reviewer checks whether the outcome actually occurred, using documentary evidence, third-party testimony, or direct observation. Second, the reviewer assesses whether the programme’s claimed contribution is plausible: did the programme activities precede the outcome, and is there a plausible causal pathway connecting them? Third, the reviewer considers alternative explanations: could the outcome have occurred without the programme’s contribution, and if so, how likely is that scenario? Wilson-Grau & Britt (2012) describe substantiation as the step that separates outcome harvesting from unstructured testimony collection. Without substantiation, outcome harvesting produces unverified contribution claims. With substantiation, it produces independently reviewed contribution evidence. The difference is significant, and evaluators who use outcome harvesting without completing the substantiation step weaken the method’s warrant considerably.

Development evaluation application: outcome harvesting for a policy advocacy programme

Wilson-Grau (2015) applied outcome harvesting to evaluate the influence of an international advocacy network on land governance policy in several Central American countries. Programme staff and partners identified 62 outcomes (changes in policies, practices, and relationships) that they attributed to the network’s advocacy activities. Independent substantiators reviewed each outcome against documentary evidence (legislative records, media reports, official correspondence) and third-party interviews. Thirty-eight outcomes were substantiated with strong evidence, sixteen were partially supported, and eight lacked sufficient corroboration. The analysis identified patterns across the substantiated outcomes: the network’s advocacy was most effective when it combined direct engagement with policymakers and evidence provision, and least effective when it relied on media campaigns alone. The graduated substantiation allowed the evaluation to produce differentiated conclusions about which advocacy strategies worked and which did not.

Outcome harvesting and contribution analysis both build contribution stories, but from opposite directions. Contribution analysis starts from the theory of change and gathers evidence to test each causal link. Outcome harvesting starts from what happened and works backward to identify which intervention activities contributed. The two methods can be combined: outcome harvesting identifies the changes, and contribution analysis tests the causal logic connecting intervention to change. In development evaluation, outcome harvesting is well suited to advocacy and policy-influence programmes where outcomes are diffuse and non-linear, to multi-stakeholder initiatives, and to complex systems interventions where the theory of change is emergent rather than pre-specified.

8.3 Strengths, limitations, and applicability

Table 8.1 summarises the two methods.

The principal strengths of participatory methods are their capacity to capture complexity, emergence, and unexpected outcomes. They centre participant voice and insider knowledge. They work at small n without requiring repeated measurement or pre-specified outcome indicators. They are less reliant on the evaluator’s prior theory about what the programme should produce.

The limitations are substantial and should not be minimised. Both methods rely on participant honesty, recall, and willingness to report. Social desirability bias can lead participants to over-attribute change to the programme. Neither method provides a formal mechanism for ruling out confounding or alternative explanations. MSC’s selection process reflects the values and power dynamics of the selecting panels. Outcome harvesting’s post-hoc attribution is vulnerable to cherry-picking if the substantiation step is weak. These limitations mean that participatory methods are rarely sufficient on their own for causal inference. They are strongest when combined with other logics: a process trace can test the mechanisms behind a harvested outcome, a diagnostic evaluation can quantify the evidential weight of a verified MSC story, and a configurational analysis can identify which contextual conditions are associated with the changes that participants report. The integration chapter (Chapter 11) discusses these combinations in detail.

The case against and for participatory causal methods

Two criticisms recur. First, stakeholder testimony is subjective and prone to social desirability bias, recall error, and strategic misreporting. Participants may attribute change to the programme because they know that is what the evaluator wants to hear, or because they want the programme to continue. Second, post-hoc attribution (identifying outcomes first and attributing them to the programme second) inverts the logic of hypothesis testing. The risk of cherry-picking favourable outcomes is real if the substantiation step is weak or absent.

Proponents respond on both points. On subjectivity, all causal inference involves judgement; participatory methods make the source of judgement explicit. When a process-tracing analyst interprets a document as evidence for a mechanism, the analyst is exercising subjective judgement too. The difference is that participatory methods locate the judgement with those who experienced the change, which gives access to information that external analysts cannot obtain. Verification and substantiation steps address (though do not eliminate) the risk of bias. On post-hoc attribution, outcome harvesting’s six-step procedure includes independent substantiation precisely to guard against cherry-picking. The method’s designers explicitly warn that omitting substantiation undermines the warrant. The participatory logic produces contribution evidence, not proof of cause and effect. Its value is as one source of causal evidence within a multi-logic evaluation design.

The methods covered in Part II so far rely on empirical evidence drawn from cases, stakeholders, or within-case analysis. The next chapter introduces a different kind of tool: computational simulation, which supports small-n evaluation not by collecting new evidence but by generating synthetic scenarios, estimating probabilities, and exploring intervention dynamics in silico.
