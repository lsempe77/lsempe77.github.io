5  Configurational Approaches

The previous chapter asked “what would have happened without the intervention?” and constructed the counterfactual through within-unit temporal comparison. This chapter asks a different question: “which combinations of conditions produced the outcome?” The multiple-causation logic recognises that development interventions rarely act alone. A conditional cash transfer programme may increase school enrolment, but only in conjunction with accessible schools, functioning payment systems, and household norms that permit girls to attend. The appropriate formalism is not the isolated difference-maker but the INUS condition introduced by Mackie (1965): an insufficient but necessary part of an unnecessary but sufficient conjunction. Configurational comparative methods (CCMs) operationalise this logic by searching for the combinations of conditions that are consistently associated with the outcome across a moderate number of cases.

Two principal methods dominate the CCM family. Qualitative comparative analysis (QCA), introduced by Ragin (1987), constructs truth tables from case-level data and applies logical minimisation to identify sufficient and necessary conditions for the outcome. Coincidence analysis (CNA), developed by Baumgartner (2009), takes a different approach: it searches the data for all minimally sufficient conditions without requiring truth-table construction or prior specification of which factor is the outcome. Both methods operationalise INUS logic, but they differ in analytical strategy, assumptions, and the role of the researcher. The choice between them has become a subject of active methodological debate.

5.1 Qualitative comparative analysis

QCA exists in three variants, each suited to different data structures. Crisp-set QCA (csQCA) codes all conditions as binary: present or absent. Fuzzy-set QCA (fsQCA) calibrates conditions to degrees of membership between 0 and 1, allowing graded distinctions (Ragin, 2008). Multi-value QCA (mvQCA) permits conditions to take more than two discrete values without requiring the continuous calibration that fsQCA demands. Table 5.1 summarises the three variants.

The analytical steps of a QCA study follow a consistent sequence regardless of variant. The analyst first identifies the conditions (potential causal factors) and the outcome, drawing on theory and case knowledge. Raw data are then calibrated into set memberships. For csQCA this means applying a threshold to classify each case as in or out of the set; for fsQCA it means specifying three qualitative anchors (full membership, crossover point, full non-membership) and using a logistic function to transform continuous data into fuzzy-set scores. The analyst constructs a truth table that lists all logically possible combinations of conditions alongside the number of observed cases and the outcome value for each row. Logical minimisation, typically using the Quine-McCluskey algorithm, reduces the truth table to the simplest set of configurations that account for the outcome. The Quine-McCluskey algorithm works by comparing pairs of configurations that differ on only one condition: if two configurations both produce the outcome and differ only on whether condition A is present, then A is logically redundant for that pair, and the algorithm removes it. This pairwise comparison is repeated until no further simplification is possible. The result takes three forms: a complex solution (no simplifying assumptions about unobserved configurations), a parsimonious solution (all logically possible simplifying assumptions), and an intermediate solution (only theoretically plausible simplifying assumptions). The complex solution is the most conservative: it reports only what the observed data support. The parsimonious solution is the most compact: it assumes that unobserved configurations would behave in whatever way produces the simplest formula. The intermediate solution occupies the middle ground: the analyst specifies which simplifying assumptions are theoretically plausible (for instance, “if political will is absent, the reform would not succeed regardless of other conditions”) and allows only those assumptions to simplify the solution. Schneider & Wagemann (2012) recommend reporting all three solutions and focusing interpretation on the intermediate solution, because it balances parsimony with theoretical defensibility.

Calibration deserves separate attention because it is the step at which substantive knowledge enters most directly. The three anchors in fsQCA are not determined by the data distribution; they are set by the analyst on the basis of theoretical or contextual knowledge about what constitutes “high” or “low” membership in a condition. A GDP-per-capita threshold for “high income” in a study of sub-Saharan African countries will differ from the same threshold in a study of OECD members. Poor calibration choices can shift cases across the crossover point and alter the truth table, changing the solution. This sensitivity is a feature of the method, not a defect: it forces the analyst to make substantive judgements explicit. But it also means that readers must be able to inspect and challenge the calibration decisions (Schneider & Wagemann, 2012).

Two types of set-theoretic relationship are central to QCA. A condition is necessary for the outcome if it is present in every case that shows the outcome. A configuration of conditions is sufficient for the outcome if every case with that configuration shows the outcome. Consistency measures the degree to which the empirical evidence supports the set-theoretic claim (perfect consistency = 1.0). Coverage measures the proportion of outcome cases that the configuration explains. Most QCA studies focus on sufficiency analysis, but necessity analysis is a distinct and important preliminary step: if a condition is necessary, it must appear in every sufficient configuration.

QCA is well suited to development evaluation questions that compare a moderate number of sites or countries. A multi-site evaluation of a governance reform implemented across 15 countries can use QCA to identify which combinations of contextual conditions (political competition, bureaucratic capacity, donor pressure, civil society strength) are associated with successful reform outcomes. A multi-component programme delivered across 20 schools can use QCA to determine which combinations of implementation features (teacher training completed, parental engagement achieved, materials delivered on time) are sufficient for improved learning outcomes. Software for QCA includes the standalone application fsQCA and the R packages QCA and SetMethods.

Development evaluation application: social accountability programmes across countries

Waddington et al. (2019) apply QCA to examine the conditions under which social accountability interventions improve public service delivery across low- and middle-income countries. The analysis covers 18 cases drawn from a systematic review of citizen engagement programmes, coding conditions such as programme design features (information provision, collective action facilitation), contextual factors (state capacity, civic space), and implementation quality. The fsQCA identifies two distinct pathways to improved service outcomes: one combining intensive information campaigns with organised citizen groups in contexts of moderate state responsiveness, and another combining direct engagement between citizens and service providers with strong programme implementation regardless of state capacity. The analysis reveals equifinality: the same outcome arises through different causal packages depending on context, a finding that a cross-case average treatment effect would have concealed.

Schneider & Wagemann (2006) apply QCA to the consolidation of democracy across post-communist states, identifying combinations of proximate and remote conditions — a canonical example of configurational analysis of governance reforms across a moderate number of countries.

5.2 Coincidence analysis

CNA differs from QCA in three respects that have practical consequences. First, CNA does not require the analyst to specify which factor is the outcome before the analysis begins. It searches for sufficient and necessary conditions across all factors simultaneously, constructing causal models bottom-up from the data (Baumgartner, 2009). Second, CNA does not construct a truth table. It identifies redundancy-free Boolean dependency structures directly, using fit parameters (consistency and coverage, computed differently from QCA) to evaluate candidate solutions. Third, CNA can model multi-outcome structures in a single analysis: if condition A is sufficient for intermediate outcome B, and B combined with C is sufficient for final outcome D, CNA can recover the entire causal chain rather than treating each outcome separately.

The practical implication is that CNA is less reliant on the analyst’s prior specification of the causal structure. When the research question is exploratory (“what causes what in this set of cases?”) rather than confirmatory (“what combinations cause this specific outcome?”), CNA has an advantage. When the causal structure may involve mediation or causal chains, CNA can detect them whereas QCA would require separate analyses for each step. The R package cna implements the method and includes tools for robustness testing.

When to prefer one method over the other depends on the evaluation question and the analyst’s prior knowledge. QCA is the stronger choice when the outcome is known, the conditions are theoretically grounded, and the analyst wants to test specific configurational hypotheses. CNA is the stronger choice when the causal structure is unknown, multiple outcomes may be causally linked, or the analyst wants to minimise the influence of prior specification on the results.

Development evaluation application: CNA for multi-outcome programme pathways

No published development evaluation has used CNA to date, reflecting the method’s recent emergence. A hypothetical application illustrates the method’s potential. A multi-country maternal health programme operates through three intermediate outcomes (trained midwives, improved supply chains, community mobilisation) that interact to produce the final outcome (reduced maternal mortality). A QCA study would require the analyst to specify each intermediate outcome as a separate analysis. CNA could analyse the full causal chain in a single model, identifying which combinations of programme activities lead to trained midwives, which combinations of trained midwives and supply chain improvements lead to community uptake, and which combinations ultimately reduce mortality. The method’s capacity to model multi-outcome causal chains makes it a natural fit for programmes with complex theories of change, provided the analyst exercises care in interpreting the data-driven results against substantive knowledge. Baumgartner & Ambühl (2020) provide the technical foundations for such an application.

5.3 The QCA-CNA debate

The relationship between QCA and CNA is not merely a matter of methodological preference; it has become a substantive dispute about what configurational methods can and cannot claim. Thiem (2022) has argued that QCA, as commonly practised, produces fragile results: solutions change substantially with small alterations in calibration thresholds, case selection, or the handling of logical remainders (unobserved configurations). Benchmarking studies using simulated data with known causal structures have shown that QCA’s truth-table approach can recover incorrect solutions when limited diversity is high, when the number of conditions is large relative to the number of cases, or when measurement error is present. These critiques target not the INUS ontology but the specific analytical procedures of truth-table construction and logical minimisation.

Baumgartner & Ambühl (2020) have argued that CNA avoids some of these problems because it does not rely on truth tables and does not require the analyst to make assumptions about unobserved configurations. They contend that CNA’s search algorithm is better suited to recovering causal structures from observational data because it tests all factors symmetrically. Proponents of QCA respond that CNA’s data-driven approach risks overfitting, that the method’s agnosticism about the outcome variable can produce causally implausible models, and that QCA’s requirement for theoretical grounding is a strength rather than a weakness (Schneider & Wagemann, 2012).

The debate remains unresolved. For the evaluator, the practical lesson is threefold. First, neither method should be applied mechanically; both require careful attention to case selection, condition specification, and robustness testing. Second, robustness checks (sensitivity analysis of calibration thresholds for QCA; variation of consistency and coverage thresholds for CNA; permutation tests for both) are not optional but essential. Third, convergence between QCA and CNA results on the same data strengthens confidence in the findings; divergence signals that the results are specification-dependent and should be interpreted cautiously.

5.4 Strengths, limitations, and applicability

The principal strengths of configurational methods are well matched to development evaluation. They handle conjunctural causation: the recognition that outcomes arise from combinations of conditions rather than single causes. They formalise equifinality: different combinations can produce the same outcome, and the methods identify all such pathways rather than estimating a single average effect. The truth tables and solution formulas are transparent and auditable. The methods operate at case numbers (10 to 50) that are common in multi-site and cross-country evaluations.

The limitations are equally important. The limited diversity problem grows rapidly with the number of conditions: five binary conditions produce 32 possible configurations, and a study with 20 cases will observe only a fraction of them. Unobserved configurations require assumptions (simplifying assumptions in QCA, or exclusion in CNA), and these assumptions can drive the results. Calibration sensitivity means that small changes in thresholds can alter the solution, particularly when cases cluster near the crossover point. Configurational methods provide no temporal or mechanistic information: they identify cross-sectional patterns but do not explain how or why a configuration produces the outcome. For mechanistic explanation, evaluators need the generative approaches discussed in the next chapter.

The case against and for configurational methods

Three criticisms recur in the methodological literature. First, QCA is deterministic in a probabilistic world: consistency thresholds below 1.0 are treated as acceptable, but the theoretical framework assumes that sufficient conditions always produce the outcome, creating a tension between the method’s logic and its practice. Second, results are fragile to specification changes: adding or removing a single condition, changing a calibration threshold, or including one additional case can alter the solution substantially. Thiem (2022) document this fragility across published QCA studies. Third, the limited diversity problem means that many solutions rely on assumptions about configurations that were never observed, and different assumptions yield different results.

Proponents respond on each point. On determinism, Schneider & Wagemann (2012) argue that consistency thresholds are an honest acknowledgement that real-world data contain noise, and that the alternative (ignoring deviations) would be worse. On fragility, robustness protocols now exist: analysts can systematically vary calibration thresholds, drop cases, and compare solutions to establish which findings are stable across specifications. On limited diversity, the distinction between complex, intermediate, and parsimonious solutions makes the role of assumptions transparent. The intermediate solution, which incorporates only theoretically plausible simplifying assumptions, offers a principled middle ground. The field has also developed formal robustness tests, including permutation-based significance tests, that were absent from early QCA practice.

Configurational methods identify which combinations of conditions produce the outcome, but they do not explain how those combinations work or why they succeed in some contexts and not others. The next chapter addresses that question through generative and mechanistic approaches: process tracing, contribution analysis, and realist evaluation.
